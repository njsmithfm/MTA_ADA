An Inaccesible System Slowly Climbs

I have mobility-limited family members, and have had to think through ways for us to get around if they were to visit me in NYC. I started looking into the MTA's accessible stations, and found that while Manhattan has the most ADA-approved stations in sheer volume, Brooklyn's ADA stations are most often to be in full working condition.

However, it should be quickly noted that in sheer numbers, Brooklyn has the lowest quantity of ADA-approved subway stations. Proportionally, all boroughs do perform consistently above 95% in the above graph, with an upward trend since 2019.

I am curious about 2022, and what could have contributed to citywide operability falling below average.

Previous lawsuits against the MTA allege that "over 80% of New York City's subway stations (360 out of 427) are not equipped with any vertical accessibility, other than stairs." An obvious and massive challenge to mobility-limited riders.

Still, these improvements can leave many people out. For my neighborhood in Brooklyn, the nearest ADA-compliant station is at least a half mile away.
